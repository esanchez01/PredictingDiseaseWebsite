Predicting Disease Risk
Through Machine Learning

An exploration into the viability of machine learning in disease risk prediction.

Authored By: Enrique Sanchez, Allston Fojas, Michael Sorenson

  • View Project

Introduction

Genetic studies have enabled researchers and medical practitioners to better understand the underlying relationships between genes and human traits. However, these traits extend to well beyond those that are not so easily seen or determined, such as disease. This relationship between genes and diseases has been investigated through Genome Wide Association Studies (GWAS), which analyze the entire human genome to find genetic variants associated with specific traits (“Genome-Wide Association Studies,” n.d.). Despite the power of these studies to analyze genetic variation, the complex nature of diseases, in addition to the environmental factors that contribute towards their development, makes it difficult to pinpoint the exact genes that lead to disease. Nonetheless, these studies have shown that the likelihood of a disease, given an individual’s set of genes, can be determined to a certain degree. Naturally, this leads to the question of how different diseases quantitatively compare in their predictability and how this can be applied to build a model that determines the likelihood of an individual to develop a particular disease. Therefore, in an effort to better understand the predictability of a disease, or a set of diseases, and how disease prediction compares across various diseases, this project will focus on building a model to address this idea using the most common form of genetic variation, Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs), and compare the effectiveness of such a model across different diseases.
